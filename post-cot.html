When does Chain-of-Thought actually fail?

My experiments on FLAN-T5 revealed something counterintuitive: more structured prompting can make things worse. Here's what actually happened in the ablation studies — and what it tells us about the limits of inference-time reasoning in encoder-decoder models.

Draft in progress. The argument is sketched out — dashed boxes mark sections still being written.

Background

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting — asking a model to reason step-by-step before answering — has become one of the most reliably cited improvements in LLM benchmarks. The original paper showed dramatic gains on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks. Since then it's been treated almost as a free lunch: add "Let's think step by step" and things get better.

My project set out to test whether that held for instruction-tuned encoder-decoder models, specifically FLAN-T5, and across a broader set of strategies: Zero-Shot, Few-Shot, standard CoT, and Self-Generated Examples (SGE).

The short answer: it doesn't always hold. And understanding why turns out to be more interesting than the benchmark numbers.

Why encoder-decoder models are different

Most CoT research is on decoder-only models — GPT-style architectures where the model generates a long completion token by token, naturally producing intermediate reasoning chains. FLAN-T5 is encoder-decoder: the input is encoded into a fixed representation, then a relatively constrained decoder produces the output.

This matters because CoT prompting assumes the model can "think in the output" — that generating reasoning tokens before the answer actually modifies the internal computation leading to that answer. In encoder-decoder models, the heavy lifting happens in the encoder pass.

What this means in practice

The takeaway isn't that CoT is bad. It's that prompting strategies are architecture-dependent, and results from decoder-only benchmarks don't automatically transfer to encoder-decoder models.

If you're working with FLAN-T5 or similar models:

  • CoT helps most on structured arithmetic tasks where the reasoning chain is short and verifiable
  • SGE is risky without a way to filter or validate the generated examples before using them
  • For commonsense tasks, Few-Shot with carefully curated examples outperformed all inference-time reasoning strategies we tested
