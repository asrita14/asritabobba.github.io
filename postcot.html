When does Chain-of-Thought actually fail?

My experiments on FLAN-T5 revealed something counterintuitive: more structured prompting can make things worse. Here's what actually happened in the ablation studies — and what it tells us about the limits of inference-time reasoning.

Background

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting — the idea of asking a model to reason step-by-step before answering — has become one of the most reliably cited improvements in LLM benchmarks. The original paper showed dramatic gains on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks. Since then it's been treated almost as a free lunch: add "Let's think step by step" and things get better.

My project set out to test whether that held for instruction-tuned encoder-decoder models, specifically FLAN-T5, and across a broader set of strategies: Zero-Shot, Few-Shot, standard CoT, and Self-Generated Examples (SGE).

The short answer: it doesn't always hold. And understanding why turns out to be more interesting than the benchmark numbers.

Why encoder-decoder models are different

Most CoT research is on decoder-only models — GPT-style architectures where the model generates a long completion token by token, naturally producing reasoning chains. FLAN-T5 is encoder-decoder: the input is encoded into a fixed representation, then decoded into an output.

This matters because CoT prompting assumes the model can "think in the output" — that generating reasoning tokens before the answer actually changes the internal computation. In encoder-decoder models, the heavy lifting happens in the encoder, and the decoder is relatively constrained in length and structure.

What this means for prompting practice

The takeaway isn't that CoT is bad. It's that prompting strategies are architecture-dependent, and results from decoder-only benchmarks don't automatically transfer.

If you're working with FLAN-T5 or similar encoder-decoder models:

  • CoT helps most on structured arithmetic tasks where the reasoning chain is short and verifiable
  • SGE is risky without a way to filter or validate the generated examples
  • For commonsense tasks, Few-Shot with carefully curated examples outperformed all inference-time reasoning strategies
